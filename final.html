Fatal Crashes Plague Philadelphia as City Earns ‘Worst US City to Drive In’ Ranking

Interpeting the city's fatal accident records over the past seven years.
by Danny Rovi

Background Information

A comprehensive analysis of fatal traffic crashes in Philadelphia from 2019 through 2025 reveals a troubling pattern of deadly incidents that mirrors the city's recent distinction as the worst city to drive in America, based on a 2025 WalletHub study, which can be found at: (https://tinyurl.com/bdv6zvhv)

Data compiled from OpenDataPhilly shows 844 fatal crashes occurred across the seven-year period, with 2020 marking the deadliest year on record at 156 fatalities, representing 18.48% of all deaths during the timeframe. The findings come as Philadelphia grapples with persistent traffic safety challenges that have culminated in its notorious national ranking. The crash data, which is refreshed nightly and maintained by the city's public data portal, paints a sobering picture of Philadelphia's roadways. Fatal incidents have remained consistently high since the 2020 peak, with 134 deaths in 2024 and 77 already recorded through 2025.

The 2020 Spike and Pandemic Driving

The dramatic surge in fatal crashes during 2020 stands as a particularly alarming data point. Despite reduced traffic volumes during pandemic lockdowns, fatalities increased by 73% compared to 2019's 90 deaths. Law enforcement officers native to the community have developed theories to help interpret this spike. Officer Matt Romberger attributes this trend to dangerous driving behaviors that emerged when emptier streets encouraged speeding and reckless behavior. “I think that some drivers took advantage of the low volume of traffic by using the streets and highways as a racetrack,” he said. “Studies have shown that extreme speeds of 30, 40, even 50mph over the speed limit were determined to be the primary factor in a lot of these fatal crashes.”

Hit-and-Run Epidemic

Perhaps one of the most disturbing factors in the analysis is the prevalence of hit-and-run crashes. Nearly 25% of all fatal incidents—209 of 844 total crashes—involved a driver who fled the scene. This percentage represents a profound breakdown in civic responsibility and complicates law enforcement efforts to hold negligent drivers accountable. Hit-and-run crashes deprive victims of immediate assistance while allowing dangerous drivers to evade consequences, even if they are eventually caught. The high rate suggests inadequacy in enforcement and penalties, as well as pointing out possible deficiencies in vehicle identification technology and witness cooperation.

Most Vulnerable Age Groups

The dataset also revealed that drivers and victims aged 31 experienced the highest number of fatal crashes, with 24 incidents across the study period. The 27 and 30 age groups tied for second with 22 fatalities each. Young adults in their 20s and 30s collectively represent a substantial portion of crash victims, raising questions about risk-taking behaviors, commuting patterns and exposure to dangerous corridors during peak traffic periods. However, the data also shows significant fatalities among senior citizens. Multiple victims in their 70s, 80s and even 90s appear in the records, highlighting the vulnerability of elderly pedestrians and drivers navigating Philadelphia's challenging street environment.

Geographic and Infrastructure Factors

Major roadways in Philadelphia dominate the crash locations throughout the dataset. Roosevelt Boulevard, long known as one of America's most dangerous roads, appears repeatedly in fatal crash records. Broad Street, Lehigh Avenue, Frankford Avenue and other high-speed corridors account for disproportionate shares of deaths. These roadways share common characteristics: multiple lanes encouraging high speeds, inadequate pedestrian infrastructure, poor lighting and design prioritizing vehicle throughput over safety. Many crashes occurred at intersections where turning vehicles struck pedestrians in crosswalks, or at mid-block locations where individuals attempted to cross wide, intimidating streets without marked crossings. The prevalence of crashes on major roads suggests systemic design failures rather than isolated incidents. Philadelphia's grid of oversized roadways, originally designed for a different era, now functions as a network of high-speed hazards cutting through residential neighborhoods.

The National Context; Looking Forward

Philadelphia's recent designation as America's worst city to drive in stems from multiple factors beyond crash data alone, including congestion, road conditions and driver behavior. However, the fatal crash statistics do provide quantitative reasoning for that qualitative assessment. The combination of aggressive driving, inadequate infrastructure, law enforcement deficiencies, and car-centric street design creates conditions where tragedy becomes routine. Unlike peer cities that have invested in new initiatives to emphasize driver safety, Philadelphia has struggled to implement coordinated safety improvements. As Philadelphia continues recording fatal crashes at elevated rates in 2025, pressure mounts for substantive action. Things like protected bike lanes, reduced speed limits, automated enforcement and redesigned intersections are lobbied for on a consistent basis by citizens. Though infrastructure improvements like this won’t eradicate fatal crashes altogether, it is a small step in the process to creating a safer city. The data from 2019 through 2025 establishes a clear baseline and identifies patterns that should inform policy responses. Whether Philadelphia can translate this evidence into meaningful change remains uncertain. For families who've lost loved ones in the 844 fatal crashes documented since 2019, the statistics represent irreplaceable people whose lives were cut short on roads that failed to protect them.
